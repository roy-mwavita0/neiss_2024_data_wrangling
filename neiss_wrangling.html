Data Wrangling of the NEISS-2024 Data

My most favorite functions in R are mutate() and select(). I believe them to be the foundation of any analytical approach. With mutate(), it offers a unique kind of flexibility, which allows me to reach out to other objects and variables to synthesize new summaries or craft temporary logic-gate variables right in the middle of a pipe. Meanwhile, select() is my go-to for structural precision, enabling me to rename columns while simultaneously filtering the dataset down to its most significant figures: select(new_name = old_name, column_name2, column_name3).

There are others, like adorn_totals(), count(name = 'new_name'), and pivot_wider(). These have been essential in creating summaries that make monitoring and reporting easier, but they also allow me to venture into learning questions in programming to maximize programmatic impact.

So, when I decided to take a stab at learning Python for data analytics and eventually data science, I went the "Tidy Way: I replicated the steps I was already used to in R language. Two aspects of Python's pandas library fully drew me in:

  1. Method Chaining (the equivalent to Rs piping) and
  2. .assign() (the equivalent to Rs mutate()).

Once I established that, I realized the rest wouldn't be hard because data cleaning and preparation take up the biggest chunk of any analytical endeavor anyway.

To sharpen these skills, I am performing a data wrangling exercise on the National Electronic Injury Surveillance System (NEISS) 2024 data. This dataset is a puzzle of raw incident reports, encoded dictionaries, and format values. My goal is to clean this raw data so we can transform encoded noise into public safety insights.

Data Wrangling

Step 1: Normalizing the Biological Clock

In data wrangling, accuracy often hinges on how we handle outliers or specialized encoding. In the NEISS dataset, age is a perfect example of a variable that requires attention.

For most patients, age is recorded in years. However, according to the neiss_disctionary, for infants the system uses a specialized 200-series code. In this system, 201 represents a 1-month-old, while 223 represents a 23-month-old. If we were to calculate a mean age without addressing this, a 1-month-old infant would appear to be a 201-year-old giant!

In[7]:
neiss_dictionary[2:6]
Out[7]:

2 Age Age Age of Patient
3 NaN NaN 0 - Age Not Recorded
4 NaN NaN 2 to 120 - Age in Years

The task is to align the ages as per the above neiss_dictionary.

Using Pythons .assign() can normalize this biological clock in a single, readable chain.

In[10]:
neiss_coded = (
    neiss_2024 # Directing our functions to the raw data
    .assign(
        correct_age = lambda x: np.where(
            # First, we identify '0' as missing/not recorded to prevent it from skewing our year-based averages.
            x['age'] == 0, np.nan, 
            np.where(
                # Next, we handle the 'month codes' (201-223) by subtracting 200 to get months, then divide by 12 
                #to convert them into year-based significant figures.
                x['age'].between(201, 223), np.trunc(((x['age'] - 200) / 12)).astype('int'), 
                x['age']
            )
        )
    )
)

In data wrangling, you never take a transformation at face value. After normalizing the ages with .assign(), we must perform an audit. It's imperative to ensure that the significant figures set to np.nan (Not a Number) are the exact same cases in the raw data (neiss_2024), which the Data Dictionary identified as "Not Recorded" (Age = 0)

The Culprits: It isn't chainsaws or fireworksit's Floors and Beds.

The Victims: The 90+ demographic is bearing a disproportionate weight of severe injury.

The Context: Clinical narratives revealed a recurring cycle of frailty and falls within care facilities.

The Timing: Risk doesn't take the weekend off, peaking on Mondays and Sundays as our household rhythms shift.
